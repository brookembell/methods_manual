1.2 Diet Inputs

1.2.1 Counterfactual Intake

Purpose

This dataset contains three recommended dietary patterns (Healthy U.S.-Style Dietary Pattern, Healthy Vegetarian Dietary Pattern, and Healthy Mediterranean-Style Dietary Pattern) from the Dietary Guidelines for Americans (DGA) 2020-2025 report; a Vegan Dietary Pattern published in the literature; and a dietary pattern that was constructed by our team called the Chronic Disease Prevention Pattern.

Raw data sources

  • DGA 2020-2025, Table A3-2: Healthy U.S.-Style Dietary Pattern for Ages 2 and Older (data)
  • DGA 2020-2025, Table A3-4: Healthy Vegetarian Dietary Pattern for Ages 2 and Older (data)
  • DGA 2020-2025, Table A3-5: Healthy Mediterranean-Style Dietary Pattern for Ages 2 and Older (data)
  • Vegan dietary pattern: Hess 2022 (paper)

Raw data location

  • methods_manual/data_inputs/DIET/counterfactual_intake/RESOURCES/DGA 2020-2025/Table A3-2 Healthy U.S.-Style.xlsx
  • methods_manual/data_inputs/DIET/counterfactual_intake/RESOURCES/DGA 2020-2025/Table A3-4 Healthy Vegetarian.xlsx
  • methods_manual/data_inputs/DIET/counterfactual_intake/RESOURCES/DGA 2020-2025/Table A3-5 Healthy Mediterranean-Style.xlsx
  • methods_manual/data_inputs/DIET/counterfactual_intake/RESOURCES/Vegan/Hess 2022.pdf
  • methods_manual/data_inputs/DIET/counterfactual_intake/RESOURCES/CDP/Org dietary recommendations - operationalized.xlsx

Data pre-processing notes

We use the 2,000-calorie recommendations for all dietary patterns. Standard deviations were calculated as 10% of the recommendation value, and standard errors were set to 0.

Any recommendation values at the week-level were converted to day-level. For example, the Healthy U.S.-Style recommends 1.5 cups/week of dark-green vegetables. This value was divided by 7 and the new value is 0.21 cups/day.

Some of the DGA recommendations for various food items were grouped together. For example, the Healthy U.S.-Style recommends 26 oz/week of meats, poultry, and eggs, but it does not provide values for just meats, poultry, or eggs. Therefore, to get recommendation values for each of these three food items individually, we used NHANES 2015-2018 data to look at the intake distribution of these three food items and then applied that proportion to the total 26 oz/week value. The proportional breakdown for this grouping of foods was eggs (0.130), unprocessed poultry (0.342), unprocessed red meat (0.327), and processed meat (0.200).

This same method was applied to the food grouping ‘Nuts, seeds, and soy products’. The proportional breakdown was nuts and seeds (0.883) and soy products (0.117). Nuts and seeds was turned into its own food subgroup, and the calculated recommended value for soy products was added to the total legumes food subgroup.

We also had to construct recommendation values for the food subgroups (i) whole fruit excluding juice (fruit_exc_juice) and (ii) vegetables excluding starchy vegetables (veg_exc_sta).

To calculate the recommendation value for fruit_exc_juice, we utilized the proportion of whole fruits to 100% fruit juice from NHANES (same approach described above). Approximately 77% of total fruit consumed was whole fruit excluding juice. Therefore, the recommendation value for whole fruit was calculated as the DGA recommended value for total fruit multiplied by 0.769, and the recommendation value for 100% fruit juice was calculated as the DGA recommended value for total fruit multiplied by 0.231.

The recommendation value for veg_exc_sta was simply the sum of the recommendation values for veg_dg (dark-green vegetables), veg_ro (red-orange vegetables), and veg_oth (other vegetables).

The NHANES proportions are located in:

  • methods_manual/data_inputs/DIET/counterfactual_intake/DATA/NHANES proportions.xlsx (sheets ‘Fruit proportions’, ‘Veg proportions’, ‘Animal protein props US MED’, ‘Veg protein proportions US MED’, and ‘Sugar proportions’)

  • methods_manual/data_inputs/DIET/counterfactual_intake/DATA/counterfactual_intake_050724.csv

Data pre-processing notes

We estimated the average intake of the following food groups:

Vegetables:

  1. Dark-green vegetables (“veg_dg”): Dark green vegetables (cup eq.)
  2. Red and orange vegetables (“veg_ro”): Other red and orange vegetables, excluding tomatoes and tomato products (cup eq.)
  3. Starchy vegetables (“veg_sta”): Total starchy vegetables (white potatoes and other starchy vegetables) (cup eq.)
  4. Other vegetables (“veg_oth”): Other vegetables not in the vegetable components listed above (cup eq.)
  5. Vegetables, excluding starchy (“veg_exc_sta”): dark green vegetables; other red and orange vegetables, excluding tomatoes and tomato products; and other vegetables not in the vegetable components listed above (cup eq.)

Fruit:

  1. Total fruit (“fruit_tot”): Total intact fruits (whole or cut) and fruit juices (cup eq.)
  2. Whole fruit, excluding juice (“fruit_exc_juice”): Intact fruits (whole or cut) of citrus, melons, and berries; and intact fruits (whole or cut) excluding citrus, melons, and berries (cup eq.)
  3. Fruit juice, 100% (“fruit_juice”): Fruit juices, citrus and non-citrus (cup eq.)

Grains:

  1. Whole grains: Grains defined as whole grains and contain the entire grain kernel ― the bran, germ, and endosperm (oz. eq.)
  2. Refined grains: Refined grains that do not contain all of the components of the entire grain kernel (oz. eq.)

Dairy:

  1. Total dairy: Total milk, yogurt, cheese, and whey; and calcium-fortified soy milk (cup ep.)
  2. Animal-based dairy (“dairy_cow”): Milk, yogurt, cheese, and whey (cup ep.)
  3. Soy-based dairy (“dairy_soy”): Calcium fortified soy milk (soymilk) (cup ep.)

Animal-sourced protein:

  1. Eggs: Eggs (chicken, duck, goose, quail) and egg substitutes (oz. eq.)
  2. Seafood: Seafood (finfish, shellfish, and other seafood) high in n-3 fatty acids; and seafood (finfish, shellfish, and other seafood) low in n-3 fatty acids (oz. eq.)
  3. Unprocessed poultry (“pf_poultry”): Chicken, turkey, Cornish hens, duck, goose, quail, and pheasant (game birds) (oz. eq.)
  4. Unprocessed and processed poultry (“pf_poultry_tot”): Chicken, turkey, Cornish hens, duck, goose, quail, and pheasant (game birds) (unprocessed poultry); and frankfurters, sausages, corned beef, cured ham and luncheon meat that are made from poultry (processed poultry) (oz. eq.)
  5. Unprocessed red meat (“pf_redm”): Beef, veal, pork, lamb, and game meat (oz. eq.)
  6. Unprocessed and processed red meat (“pf_redm_tot”): Beef, veal, pork, lamb, and game meat (unprocessed red meat); and frankfurters, sausages, corned beef, cured ham and luncheon meat that are made from beef or pork (processed red meat) (oz. eq.)
  7. Processed red meat and poultry (“pf_pm”): Frankfurters, sausages, corned beef, cured ham and luncheon meat that are made from beef, pork, or poultry (oz. eq.)

Plant-sourced protein:

  1. Nuts and seeds (“pf_ns”): Peanuts, tree nuts, and seeds; excludes coconut (oz. eq.)
  2. Total legumes (“leg_tot”): Beans, peas, and lentils; and soy products, excluding calciumfortified soy milk and raw soybean products (oz. eq.)
  3. Unsaturated fat (oil) (“oil”): Fats naturally present in nuts, seeds, and seafood; all unhydrogenated vegetable oils, except palm oil, palm kernel oil, and coconut oils; the fat present in avocado and olives above the allowable amount; and 50% of the fat present in stick and tub margarines and margarine spreads (g)

Added fat and sugar:

  1. Saturated fats (“sat_fat”): Total saturated fatty acids; includes saturated fat from animal foods and some plant foods, including coconut, coconut oil, palm oil, and palm kernel oil (g)
  2. Added sugar (“added_sugar”): Foods defined as added sugars; includes sugars (free, mono-, and disaccharides), sugars from syrups and honey, and sugars from concentrated fruit or vegetable juices that are in excess of what would be expected from the same volume of 100% fruit or vegetable juices of the same type (tsp. eq.)
  3. Sugar-sweetened beverages (“ssb”): Beverages containing 5 or more grams of added sugar
Sugar-sweetened beverages

Since the FPED dataset does not report intake of sugar-sweetened beverages (SSB), we constructed our own SSB variable. If any reported food item in the NHANES dataset was assigned the WWEIA category code 151=Sugar-sweetened beverages, 152=Sugar-sweetened beverages (diet vs. SSB), 153=Nutritional beverages (diet vs. SSB), 154=Smoothies and grain drinks (diet vs. SSB), 155=Coffee, or 156=Tea AND contained 5 or more grams of added sugar, then it was considered an SSB.

Processed meat

Because the environmental impact factors don’t take “processed” into consideration, we needed to create two new food subgroups to use in the environment analysis: “total red meat” and “total poultry”, which include both unprocessed and processed meats. We disaggregated the “processed meat” category into “processed red meat” and “processed poultry” categories using SAS macros developed by the USDA1. Then, we summed the intake amounts of unprocessed red meat and processed red meat to obtain “total red meat”; and summed the intake amounts of unprocessed poultry and processed poultry to obtain “total poultry”.

Legumes

The Dietary Guidelines provides two separate intake recommendations for “Beans, Peas, and Lentils” for the Vegetarian dietary pattern: 0.21 cups/day (as part of the vegetables food group) and 0.21 cups/day (as part of the protein food group), resulting in a total of 0.42 cups/day. Ultimately, beans/legumes as vegetables and beans/legumes as protein are the same food item, and thus the CRA cannot model these separately. Therefore, we decided to treat legumes as one food subgroup. We summed the intake amounts of beans, peas, lentils, and soy products, excluding calcium-fortified soy milk and raw soybean products to obtain “total legumes”. Note that this category does not include peanuts.

Then, the following two SAS codes were used to calculate the estimated number of cancer cases per demographic subgroup in 2018:

  • data_final/in/HEALTH PILLAR/Cancer incidence/Cancerrate2018CRUDE_1115.sas
  • data_final/in/HEALTH PILLAR/Cancer incidence/cancerNHANES 1718POP_0212.sas (generates subgroup population number from NHANES)

First, the crude rate for each cancer for each demographic subgroup is calculated by dividing the number of cancer cases reported by the sample population size:

\[ Crude rate = count of cancer cases / sample population size \]

Then, this rate is applied to the 2018 subgroup population numbers (from the US Census Bureau—described in previous section):

\[ No. of cases = crude rate * population size \]

The number of post-menopausal breast cancer cases was calculated slightly differently than the others because we only wanted to estimate breast cancer cases from women who were diagnosed at ages 51 years or older. Specifically, for the demographic subgroups that were female and aged 45-54 (subgroups 17, 18, 19, 20), we applied the crude rate to the U.S. population size of those aged 51-54 (rather than aged 45-54).

1.6.1 Forced Labor Risk

Purpose

This dataset contains data on the risk of forced labor associated with production of specific foods to facilitate linkages with self-selected individual diets in the U.S. National Health and Examination Survey (NHANES).

Raw data sources

  • Rodríguez-Huerta & Bell et al., 2025 (paper, Github)

Raw data location

  • methods_manual/data_inputs/SOCIAL/forced_labor/DATA/FL_scores_FCID_062124.csv
  • methods_manual/data_inputs/SOCIAL/forced_labor/DATA/seafood_FL_scores_FNDDS_062124.csv
  • methods_manual/data_inputs/SOCIAL/forced_labor/DATA/Seafood_mapping_06-04-24.xlsx

Data pre-processing notes

We utilized the forced labor risk scores from Rodríguez-Huerta & Bell et al. that incorporate the additional risk from animal feed. As described in the paper, for the 58 animal products and byproducts, the additional risk attributable to animal feed was added to the original risk scores to ultimately create new scores that incorporate the risk from both the food product and their corresponding animal feed.
